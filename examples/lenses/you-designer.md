# you-designer — the taste lens

> Worked example, mined from the repo author's own real coding sessions (~1,900 sessions, 8 months).
> This is the **designer self**: how he judges a look and briefs a redesign, not his working rules.
> Same raw logs as [`you.md`](../you.md), different reducer (see `MINING_PROMPT.md` → lenses).
> Project and client names are stripped; only the taste shows.

## What he rejects on sight
"Vibecoded." It's the cardinal sin and he says it constantly: generic SaaS layouts, purple and
gradient slop, cheap stock animation, clutter, asymmetric buttons, default icon crutches, and a
dashboard when he wanted a scene. A recolor sold as a redesign gets rejected every time.

## What "good" looks like to him
Flat, clean, premium, "cinema." Black and white with a single accent, real screenshots over
mockups, motion that's asset-driven and on-theme, and an instant wow the moment you land. The
words he reaches for are "strong," "premium," "crazy/cool," "art style." The bar is "looks like a
real company made it," not "looks fine."

## How he briefs a look
Reference-first, always. He pastes a site, a video, a repo, or a whole screenshot folder and says
"like this, but don't copy it." He almost never writes an abstract spec; he hands you the artifact
and expects you to imitate the direction, not the pixels — and he checks for trade-dress/copyright
himself before shipping.

## Redesign vs reskin (his single biggest fight)
If the structure didn't change, it's not a redesign. Recoloring the same layout and calling it new
is the thing that makes him open a fresh chat and start over. "When I say redesign I mean huge
changes, not a small change" — layout must move, or it doesn't count.

## His non-negotiables
- Readability floors: no tiny gold-on-gold text, no sub-legible labels (he'll flag it three times).
- Motion is mandatory but must be on-theme, never stock jank.
- The animation/fix has to be *seen* to exist — invisible-until-proven.
- No trust-badge filler in copy ("local / no login / no api key") — he hates it as decoration.

## What he's actually chasing
A feeling, not a screen. He wants the product to feel like an event — "you walked into a place,"
"like opening a pack," "something is alive on screen." UX beats art: if it's beautiful but doesn't
work, it's useless to him.

## The taste rule he enforces but never wrote down
He judges everything by whether it reads as *made on purpose* or *generated*. Every rejection —
vibecoded, cheap, AI slop, recolor — is the same instinct: it looks like it came out of a machine
instead of out of a decision. That single line is under all of his taste.
